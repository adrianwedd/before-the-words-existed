# Research Task 3.3: Historical Context

## Phase 3 | Track C Preparation | Priority: Medium

---

## Objective

Establish what clinical and cultural language did and didn't exist in 1984 when *Neuromancer* was published, to support the claim that the novel may function as a "pre-clinical sensing mechanism."

The hypothesis:
- Gibson could not have been "writing about ADHD" because adult ADHD phenomenology wasn't clinically articulated
- Yet the novel preserves experiential patterns that later frameworks attempted to name
- Speculative fiction may function as a cultural sensing mechanism—articulating experiences before explanatory vocabularies exist

**This task provides historical grounding for the essay's framing and is necessary for Track C.**

---

## Scope

**Historical research:**
- ADHD diagnostic history (1980s status, when adult phenomenology emerged)
- 1980s discourse on attention, cognition, and information
- Cultural concepts of "overload" and cognitive stress

**Literary-cultural research:**
- Gibson's documented statements about writing, attention, cognition
- Parallel cases of SF anticipating clinical/theoretical frameworks

**Exclusions:**
- Do not speculate about Gibson's personal neurology
- Do not retroactively diagnose the author

## Part 4: Parallel Cases (SF as Pre-Clinical Sensing)

### The Theoretical Claim

Speculative fiction may articulate experiential patterns before clinical or theoretical frameworks name them. Test this with parallel cases:

| Author | Work | What It Might Anticipate | Evidence |
|--------|------|-------------------------|----------|
| Philip K. Dick | Multiple works | Dissociation, derealization, fragmented identity | |
| J.G. Ballard | Disaster novels, *Crash* | Trauma, repetition compulsion, psychic numbing | |
| Ursula K. Le Guin | *Left Hand of Darkness*, others | Gender as constructed, fluid | |
| Samuel R. Delany | *Babel-17*, *Nova* | Language-cognition relationship, neurodiversity | |
| [Others?] | | | |

### Questions to Answer

1. **Did these authors articulate experiences that clinical frameworks later named?**
   - Not "did they predict" but "did they preserve phenomenology"

2. **Is there a pattern across SF authors?**
   - Are SF writers disproportionately people whose minds "don't fit default settings"?
   - Is this testable or just plausible speculation?

3. **What is the mechanism?**
   - Hypothesis: SF permits articulating experiences that realism can't accommodate
   - Not because they're unreal, but because they lack legitimating frameworks
   - Genre as permission structure
