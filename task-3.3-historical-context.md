## Objective

Establish what clinical and cultural language did and didn't exist in 1984 when *Neuromancer* was published, to support the claim that the novel may function as a "pre-clinical sensing mechanism."

The hypothesis:
- Gibson could not have been "writing about ADHD" because adult ADHD phenomenology wasn't clinically articulated
- Yet the novel preserves experiential patterns that later frameworks attempted to name
- Speculative fiction may function as a cultural sensing mechanism—articulating experiences before explanatory vocabularies exist

**This task provides historical grounding for the essay's framing and is necessary for Track C.**

### 3. 1980s Attention Discourse Summary (300-400 words)
The early 1980s offered multiple partial vocabularies for attention and cognitive stress, but no stable public language for adult ADHD phenomenology. Clinically, DSM-III (1980) reframed the problem as **Attention Deficit Disorder**, yet adult persistence remained marginal, confined to “ADD, Residual Type.” Research hubs like Paul Wender’s Utah group used criteria that already included affective lability and disorganization, but this remained research‑local rather than cultural common sense. In other words, a clinician *could* diagnose an adult as residual ADD, but the category was thin and structurally backward‑looking.

Outside psychiatry, attention problems were externalized into technology and work. In 1984, Craig Brod’s *Technostress* named a “disease of adaptation,” framing difficulty concentrating, irritability, fatigue, and overload as symptoms of the computer age rather than traits of the individual. Sherry Turkle’s *The Second Self* (also 1984) described the “holding power” of computers and the appeal of a rule‑governed world, effectively documenting hyperfocus as context‑dependent rather than pathological. Alvin Toffler’s “information overload” and “future shock” discourse further normalized the idea that the environment—not the person—was the problem. Workplace labels like “burnout” offered a corporate vocabulary for executive collapse without invoking neurodevelopmental difference.

This yields a clear historical picture for 1984: there *was* a nascent clinical term (ADD), an emergent research‑level adult phenotype (Utah Criteria), and a cultural discourse of overload and technostress. What did *not* exist was a stable neurotype vocabulary that could coherently name adult attentional mismatch as an interface‑specific cognitive style. Adult ADD was a “residual” afterimage of childhood disorder; technostress and overload treated the mind as an overwhelmed normal. This fragmented vocabulary is precisely the “negative space” in which *Neuromancer* operates: the phenomenology is present, but the words are scattered across incompatible frames.

### 6. "Pre-Clinical Sensing" Theoretical Sketch (300-400 words)
- What is the proposed mechanism?
- Why might SF function this way?
- What are the limits of this claim?

**Sketch (now supported by research set):**  
The research establishes a fractured 1984 vocabulary: a thin clinical category (ADD with a residual adult category), a research‑level adult phenotype (Utah Criteria), and a cultural discourse of technostress, overload, and hacker hyperfocus. In that fragmented field, speculative fiction can act as a *permission structure*: it renders experiential patterns that lack a stable name by relocating them into interface metaphors (cyberspace, simstim, console), allowing readers and writers to perceive them without clinical authorization.

The 1984 coincidence sharpens this claim: *Neuromancer*, Brod’s *Technostress*, Turkle’s *The Second Self*, and Levy’s *Hackers* all appear in the same year, suggesting a multi‑channel cultural sensing of attention, interface, and cognition rather than a single author’s idiosyncratic insight.

This is not prediction but *phenomenological preservation*. PKD provides the clearest parallel: he explicitly uses fiction to “formulate perception,” mapping dissociation and temporal instability decades before those terms stabilized in popular discourse. Early *Neuromancer* criticism reveals the same problem from another angle: critics reach for schizophrenia, addiction, postmodern fragmentation, and gnostic longing because the vocabulary for interface‑dependent cognition did not yet exist. The mechanism, then, is double: SF licenses descriptions of atypical cognition (PKD) and produces new interpretive vocabularies for readers (cyberpunk criticism), both operating in the absence of a settled clinical lexicon.

Limits remain. The claim is not that SF “diagnoses” or “foretells” clinical frameworks. It is that SF can *stage* experiences for which clinical language is late, contested, or incomplete. The argument should explicitly acknowledge this distinction and avoid causal claims about influence.
