## Reviewer A: The Phenomenology Purist

### The Strongest Objection

"Mismatch" is not a phenomenological category—it is a disability studies import dressed in Husserlian clothing. Phenomenology describes the *structure of experience as it presents itself to consciousness*; it does not measure one cognitive mode against another and declare a "fit." The essay performs a diagnostic operation (measuring Case against an environment and finding the fit inadequate) while claiming to bracket diagnosis. This is not epoché; this is disability studies wearing a false beard.

### Supporting Pressure Points

- **The "pre-clinical sensing" hypothesis is unfalsifiable.** The essay claims SF "preserves phenomenology before clinical vocabularies stabilize" (§3, §10). But what would count as *failing* to preserve such phenomenology? If Gibson wrote different prose, would the hypothesis be rejected, or would the essay simply not have been written? This is selection bias disguised as method.

- **The "reversibility" criterion confuses phenomenology with behaviorism.** The essay tests whether "the state changes with access" (§3)—but that is a functional, third-person criterion, not a first-person description of how experience presents itself. Husserl would not recognize this as phenomenological reduction.

- **The essay smuggles in normativity.** Phrases like "cognition coheres" versus "collapses" (§1), "traction" versus "grinding mismatch" (§4)—these are evaluative, not descriptive. The essay assumes a teleology in which Case *should* have access, and deprivation is a falling-short. That is not phenomenology; that is covert advocacy for a particular mode of being.

- **The body/embodiment section (§5) confuses interoception with phenomenology.** Cataloging sweat, nausea, and hunger is empirical description, not eidetic analysis. Where is the account of how the body is *constituted* in consciousness? The essay treats the body as a datum, not as a constitutive horizon.

- **The Merleau-Ponty absence.** If this were a serious phenomenology of embodiment, it would engage *Phenomenology of Perception*. The claim that the body "becomes a prison" when the interface is absent is precisely the kind of claim Merleau-Ponty's motor intentionality could illuminate—but the essay doesn't cite him, relying instead on "interoceptive mismatch" (a psychological term, not a phenomenological one).

### The Steelman Version

A defender might argue: (1) The essay explicitly disclaims strict Husserlian method and uses "phenomenology" in the broader sense of "first-person experiential description." (2) The reversibility criterion is a *discriminant* to separate the mismatch structure from noir mood, not a phenomenological reduction in the technical sense. (3) The normativity is Case's own—the text itself frames deprivation as loss.

**Is this adequate?** Partially. The essay could simply stop calling itself "phenomenological" and call itself "experiential-structural," which would be more defensible. But the credentialing function of "phenomenology" is doing rhetorical work the essay relies on. The steelman holds if the reader accepts a loose sense of the term; it fails if the reader expects disciplinary precision.

### Verdict

**Wounds but does not kill.** The essay can survive by softening its phenomenological claims or explicitly acknowledging its hybrid method. But the disciplinary imprecision is a real vulnerability, and a hostile reader could use it to dismiss the project's credibility.

## Reviewer B: The Disability Studies Skeptic

### The Strongest Objection

The essay romanticizes cognitive difference by treating it as a "superpower" that merely needs the "right interface." The mismatch model lets the author have it both ways: acknowledge suffering in deprivation (so it's not Pollyanna) while framing the matrix as the "true" home of Case's cognition (so it's not tragic). This is the "unmasking" move that disability studies has criticized for decades: neurodivergence as latent specialness awaiting the correct accommodation. Real ADHD phenomenology includes executive dysfunction that does not resolve at any interface—a fact the essay conveniently ignores.

### Supporting Pressure Points

- **The two-dependency model (§7) is suspiciously convenient.** Drug addiction is "real" and bad; matrix-dependency is "functional" and morally neutral. On what principled basis? The essay cites "procedural longing" versus "hedonic cycling" but this distinction is imposed, not demonstrated. Why isn't matrix-dependency also pathological? Because the author needs it not to be.

- **The "institutional misdescription" thesis (§7) is unfalsifiable.** If Armitage calls Case's dependency a problem, that's "institutional control." If Case calls himself an addict, that's "localized to stimulants." Any evidence that contradicts the mismatch model is reclassified as misdescription. This is a classic immunization strategy.

- **The essay does not engage lived-experience scholarship.** Where is Russell Barkley on executive function? Where is CHADD's phenomenological archive? The essay treats ADHD as a literary analogy but never asks whether actual ADHD experience maps onto Case's profile. Domain-specific hyperfocus exists, yes—but so does task-switching failure, working memory collapse, and rejection-sensitive dysphoria. The essay cherry-picks the traits that fit.

- **The Molly contrast (§6) pathologizes her, too.** By framing her as "embodied cognition" optimized for physical action, the essay implies that both Case and Molly are "mismatch" subjects. If everyone is a mismatch case, the concept loses explanatory force. Or does the essay think neurotypicality simply doesn't exist in this novel? If so, it should say so explicitly.

- **The "recognition" structure is appropriative.** The essay gestures at neurodiversity language (§2) but uses it for literary prestige without accountability. Neurodiversity activists have critiqued exactly this move: academics treating clinical categories as aesthetic resources while bracketing lived consequences. "We don't diagnose" is a convenient disclaimer that lets the essay eat its cake.

### The Steelman Version

A defender might argue: (1) The essay repeatedly emphasizes that deprivation is "attritional" and "grinding," not a superpower narrative. (2) The two-dependency model is textually derived, not imposed—the novel does distinguish drug scenes from matrix scenes. (3) The essay explicitly declines to diagnose and offers "recognition without reduction" (§12), which is precisely the move responsible scholarship requires.

**Is this adequate?** The defense is internally coherent, but it leaves the appropriation critique unanswered. The essay uses neurodivergence as an interpretive lens without ever putting its claims to the test of actual neurodivergent phenomenology. That asymmetry is real.

### Verdict

**Wounds significantly.** The essay's rhetorical positioning depends on a neurodiversity framing that it never fully earns. A revision should either engage lived-experience literature directly or retreat from the neurodivergent analogy altogether. As written, the essay borrows credibility from disability studies without paying rent.

## Reviewer C: The SF Formalist / Genre Specialist

### The Strongest Objection

The "naming gap" thesis strawmans earlier critics. The essay claims Bukatman, Hayles, and Jameson "could not name" interface-dependent cognition (§9)—but they did name it, using different terms: "terminal identity," "flickering signifier," "schizophrenic temporality." These are not "approximate names" for a truth the essay finally captures; they are different analytic objects. The essay's method does not reveal what others missed; it asks a different question and claims priority for its answer.

### Supporting Pressure Points

- **Bukatman's "terminal identity" already includes reversibility.** His argument is that the cyberpunk subject *depends on* technological mediation for coherence. That is precisely the mismatch model's claim. The essay acknowledges this (§9) but then asserts that Bukatman lacked the "finer instrument." What, exactly, does the mismatch model explain that "terminal identity" does not?

- **The noir reading is rigged.** The essay admits that noir "explains atmosphere" (§11) but claims it cannot explain "procedural longing" or "immediate restoration of agency." But noir criticism never claimed to be a phenomenological theory of cognition. The discriminants (reversibility, precision, relationality) are designed to find noir lacking. This is not testing a hypothesis; it is stacking the deck.

- **The Molly contrast relies on a selective reading of genre convention.** Molly's "embodied cognition" is a standard action-heroine trope: the lethal female body. The essay treats her as a "cognitive ecology" foil, but the novel is also just doing pulp storytelling. The simstim sequences are spectacle; reading them as phenomenological data requires interpretive labor the essay does not justify.

- **The 1984 coincidence is weak.** Brod, Turkle, and Levy are cited as evidence that Gibson was "in resonance with an ambient diagnostic field" (§2). But resonance is not causation, and Gibson's own interviews suggest he was writing about drugs, capitalism, and alienation—not cognitive neuroscience. The historical framing imposes coherence on a coincidence.

- **The Dick parallel (§10) is a liability, not an asset.** Including Dick expands the argument's exposure without increasing its explanatory power. If SF generally "senses" phenomenology pre-clinically, then the claim is unfalsifiable: any SF text that resonates with later frameworks becomes evidence. The argument needs scope limits, not expansion.

### The Steelman Version

A defender might argue: (1) The essay explicitly states it does not "displace" or "replace" Bukatman and Hayles (§3, §9). (2) The noir discriminants are falsifiable—if Case showed stable engagement in deprivation, the model would fail. (3) The Molly reading is grounded in simstim specifics (the "jolt into other flesh," the agency loss), not in generic action-hero tropes.

**Is this adequate?** The defense on points (1) and (2) is reasonable. But the critic is right that the essay's claim to novelty is oversold. "A finer instrument" is not the same as a different instrument. A more honest framing would be: "Here is a specific phenomenological lens that complements the existing literature," not "Here is what they missed."

### Verdict

**Grazes but does not wound seriously.** The essay's engagement with the critical literature is real, and the discriminants are methodologically defensible. But the rhetoric of "naming gap" and "could not name" overstates the case. A revision should tone down the novelty claims and position the argument as additive rather than corrective.

## 5. Methodological Critique

The evidence-logging system (Task IDs, voice-channel tags) creates an **illusion of empiricism** while hiding interpretive choices. Tagging a passage as "Free Indirect" versus "Narrator" is itself an interpretation; the essay treats these tags as raw data. The Task IDs suggest a database, but the database is not supplied; the reader cannot verify coding decisions. Worse, the tags are not systematically applied: some footnotes include IDs, others do not. The method looks rigorous but is operationally opaque.

Additionally, the "discriminants" (reversibility, precision, relationality) are not derived from a theory; they are *invented* to distinguish mismatch from noir. The essay does not explain why these three criteria are the right ones, or how they would apply to other texts. This is ad hoc methodology dressed as systematic procedure.

## 6. Historical Critique

The historical claims are **mostly accurate but occasionally imprecise**:

- **DSM-III and ADD Residual Type:** Accurate. The residual label did require childhood hyperactivity as a gate, and adult phenomenology was underdeveloped.
- **Utah Criteria:** The citation is vague ("Wender et al., 1980s"). The Utah Criteria were formalized in published work (1981, 1985), and the essay should cite specific publications. The phrasing "in research enclaves" implies marginality, but Wender's group was actually prominent.
- **Neurodiversity timeline:** Accurate. The 1983 Social Model, 1993 Sinclair, 1998 Singer/Blume dating is correct. However, the essay elides the fact that autism and ADHD communities developed different neurodiversity frameworks; treating them as a single movement is a simplification.
- **"Before the words existed":** This phrase is the essay's title claim, but the words *did* exist in fragments (technostress, ADD, hacker obsession). The claim is that no *unified* vocabulary existed; a subtler point that should be stated more precisely.

**Verdict:** No fatal errors, but the historical apparatus should be tightened. The Utah Criteria citation needs specificity, and the neurodiversity timeline should acknowledge internal diversity.

## 7. Rhetorical Critique

The "labcoat with collar up" tone is a **self-protective performance**. The essay hedges weak claims ("the claim is modest but decisive," "not diagnostic," "not universality, only coherence") while making strong claims elsewhere ("the naming gap is visible," "the text preserves the structure anyway," "the lab coat stays on"). This asymmetry allows the essay to retreat to humility when challenged while claiming victory in the conclusion.

Specific rhetorical moves:

- **"The essay follows the evidence"** is a claim to objectivity that the essay has not earned. All essays select evidence.
- **"The collar is up"** is a stylistic flourish that substitutes attitude for argument.
- **"Recognition without reduction"** is a formula that sounds ethical but is operationally vague. Recognition by whom? For what purpose?
- **Repeated use of "therefore" and "this is why"** creates a logical surface that is not always supported by the preceding material.

**Verdict:** The tone is confident enough to irritate a hostile reader and cautious enough to evade direct refutation. This is effective rhetoric, but it should be acknowledged as rhetoric rather than presented as scientific detachment.

## 8. The Missing Counter-Reading

The strongest alternative reading the essay does NOT engage is: **Case is a junkie, and the matrix is his drug.**

This reading takes seriously that the novel is *about addiction*—not as a double helix of separate dependencies, but as a unified structure. Case's longing for the matrix is the longing of an addict for his fix; the restoration scene is a hit; the prose that follows is the rush. The "procedural precision" the essay identifies is the addict's ritualistic attention to paraphernalia. The "homecoming" is the addict's sense that the drug is his "true self."

This reading explains everything the mismatch model explains, without requiring the conceptual apparatus of interface-dependent cognition. It has additional textual support: Gibson explicitly framed Case as an addict in interviews, and the novel's structure mirrors the addiction narrative (loss, seeking, temporary restoration, depletion).

**The essay's two-dependency model is an attempt to defuse this reading**, but the defusal is not convincing. The essay distinguishes "hedonic cycling" from "procedural access" on the basis of phenomenological signatures, but the addict's longing can also be procedural (anticipation, preparation, execution). The drug-as-drug and matrix-as-drug readings are not cleanly separable.

**Is this counter-reading stronger than the mismatch model?** It is at least equally strong. The essay should engage it more directly rather than dismissing it as "conflation."
